# Writing Playbook

Use this file after the figure logic is clear. It supports titles, captions, legends, and concise figure-facing prose.

## Core stance

Polishing is not only about style. It is also about making the reasoning legible.

## Hourglass structure

Most strong research writing follows a broad -> narrow -> broad pattern:

- Introduction: open the territory, narrow to the gap, then state the study
- Discussion/Conclusion: start from the specific findings, then widen to implications and limits

## Section responsibilities

### Introduction
- What is already known?
- What remains unresolved?
- What exact question does this study ask?
- How does the study address it?

### Results
- State what was observed.
- Give the object, condition, quantitative support, and direct result.
- Do not add long mechanistic interpretation.

### Discussion
- Explain what the findings mean.
- Compare with earlier work fairly.
- Note plausible explanations and limitations.
- Hedging belongs here.

### Methods
- Methods should pass a reproducibility test.
- Avoid vague phrases such as `under standard conditions` or `using routine methods`.

### Conclusion
- Restate the central contribution.
- Identify the decisive evidence.
- State the implication with a boundary.
- Do not introduce new data.

### Abstract
- context or problem
- gap
- approach
- key result
- implication

## Style guardrails

- Prefer cautious, precise prose.
- Avoid contractions.
- Use British spelling by default.
- Define abbreviations on first use.
- Keep titles and figure legends concise.
- Do not invent references, data, mechanisms, or novelty claims.
- Do not upgrade association to causation.

## Phrase families

### Evidence strength
- strong: show, demonstrate, establish, reveal, identify
- moderate: suggest, indicate, support the view that, are consistent with, point to
- speculative: may reflect, could arise from, appears to, seems likely, might be explained by

### Transition families
- contrast: however, by contrast, nevertheless, despite this, whereas
- addition: furthermore, in addition, moreover, also
- consequence: therefore, thus, consequently, as a result, thereby
- qualification: notably, importantly, approximately, in part, at least in this cohort

### Gap language
- remains poorly understood
- has not been examined in
- has received limited attention
- few studies have addressed
- evidence remains sparse for

### Limitation language
- these findings should be interpreted with caution because
- a limitation of this study is that
- the generalisability of these results is limited by
- we cannot exclude the possibility that
- another source of uncertainty is

### Future-work language
- further work is needed to determine whether
- future studies should examine
- a useful next step would be to
- larger studies are required to validate

## Output preference

If text is polished, return the polished text plus a few brief revision notes. Keep the prose concise and avoid overstating the evidence.
